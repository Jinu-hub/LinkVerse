## Introduction: A paradox from a year of using AI in real work

It has been a little over a year since I started using **AI for development** in earnest.

I want to put down some of what I have felt along the way, in a personal, informal way.

I am still very much in a trial-and-error phase, so this is less a finished conclusion than an experience-based snapshot of where I stand now.

Lately, a lot of teams are trying to use AI to get more out of their development work, and I think that direction is a good one.

At the same time, one thing feels almost paradoxical.

Bringing in AI does not seem to lift everyone’s productivity in a flat, even way. Your existing skills and experience matter, of course—but **whether you have a mindset for meeting AI head-on** matters even more, and that ends up creating a large gap in outcomes.

---

## Understanding shapes the quality of the output

Think about turning an English source into Japanese.

The same English sentence, asked of AI by someone who barely knows Japanese and by someone who is comfortable in Japanese, can end up in very different places.

Of course, AI is a strong tool: in a few seconds it can produce a translation that reads naturally.

For everyday conversation or a quick grasp of meaning, that can be enough.

But when you move into areas where accuracy and nuance matter—business documents, reports, quotes, briefing materials—the picture shifts.

Then it matters less whether something was “translated” at all, and more about things like:

- Flow of the sentences
- Word choices
- Order of ideas
- Subtle tone and nuance
- The impression left on the reader

In the end, quality is not decided by the first draft AI gives you.

It depends on how you read it against your own bar, what you ask for next, and how far you can refine it.

Someone who does not read Japanese will struggle to do that fine tuning.

Someone with a solid feel for the language can use the draft as a starting point and, through repeated feedback, push it toward the level they want.

---

## Reference points and the productivity gap

I see something similar in development.

People who understand the goal clearly and carry a mental picture of the process, plus a sense of what “good” looks like, tend to get more out of AI.

For example, they can judge:

- How to split the work
- What to do and what not to do
- What counts as a good outcome
- Where to let AI run and where a human has to decide
- Whether generated code fits the current architecture

With that kind of bar, you do not simply accept AI output—you keep feeding it back and improving it.

Without enough of that grounding, AI can still get you to something that *looks* plausible in a fairly short time.

The harder part is what comes after: pushing quality further often hits a wall.

There is no question that AI has lowered the bar to *starting* development, and that is a real positive.

In day-to-day work, though, I also feel the gap between “how it looks on the surface” and “how well it is actually understood” can show up as a bigger risk than before.

---

## AI as an amplifier

To me, AI is less a flat “leveling” tool and, in a sense, more like an **amplifier**.

For people who already have a bar, it helps them act on that bar faster.

For people with design sense, it helps them turn that direction into something concrete faster.

On the other hand, when internal understanding is thin, plausible output can stack up quickly—and when you later have to explain, change, or extend the system, the pain can grow.

The gap between “does the code run?” and “does it break architectural consistency, and are there security issues?” is a full gulf in terms of output quality.

There is also a big difference between “fix this for me” and “this part should go through shared logic, so please split the service layer from the transform layer and clean it up.”

AI moves from the standards and language the person giving instructions already has.

So I think the productivity gap between people who can give concrete feedback and people who cannot is something that can widen over time.

AI-generated code can work right away.

But code stacked up without a real grasp of what is inside can turn into **black-box technical debt** that future you has to live with.

---

## How teams are changing, and the junior growth problem

I have also heard that some companies, after seeing how useful AI can be, are trying to hire fewer new graduates.

The story seems to be: a smaller group of experienced people with strong design skills, using AI aggressively, can reach a certain level of throughput without needing as large a headcount as before.

That shift is probably not only about cost.

If AI is harnessed with control, you can hope for better consistency across the system and better code quality overall.

That trend also touches how developers grow.

Junior developers used to build intuition about systems by repeating simpler tasks over time.

When AI takes over part of that space, organizations face a new question:

**How do we grow junior developers in an AI-heavy world.**

Fewer grunt tasks can be a win, but it can also mean fewer footholds for building fundamentals.

So going forward, I think we need to think not only about adopting AI but about how people can still grow inside environments built around it.

---

## Using AI is ultimately about judgment

I am still experimenting myself.

Sometimes prompting AI feels hard; often the output is not what I hoped.

That is especially true when I step into a new area without enough background knowledge.

At times it feels almost as hard as explaining my thoughts clearly to another person.

Even so, one thing has gotten clearer through those misses.

The core of using AI well is not only “using a good tool.” It is **what bar you bring**, **what direction you steer feedback**, and **where a human still has to decide**.

AI is less something that hands you the right answer and more a tool that probabilistically serves up plausible-looking answers.

So the final call is still on people.

---

## Closing

AI can lift productivity for many people.

But I do not think the effect shows up the same way for everyone.

For some it becomes real leverage; in other cases it can pile up plausible-looking complexity that nobody truly understands.

So in development ahead, what matters may not be “knowing how to use AI” alone.

These may matter more:

- Structuring problems
- A bar for judging output
- The ability to give specific feedback
- The ability to read generated code and own it
- The strength to set direction between people and AI

Right now, I see AI not as a replacement for development but as something that **amplifies a developer’s standards and way of thinking**.

It is not a final answer, but after this year, that is how it feels to me.

AI can be a leveling tool and, at the same time, a polarizing one.

And that difference, in the end, starts from the bar each of us already holds.
