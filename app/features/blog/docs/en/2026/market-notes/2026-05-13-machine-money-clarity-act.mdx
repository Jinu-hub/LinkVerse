## May 14: Will Money for Machines Become Legal?

Tomorrow, May 14.  
The U.S. Senate Banking Committee is expected to hold a committee vote, a Markup, on the CLARITY Act.

Whether this drives a rise or fall in overall crypto markets matters a lot,  
but I want to carefully organize some thoughts about the deeper design questions behind it.

This bill will not change everything overnight,  
but this trend does seem to point in a clear direction.

Digital assets no longer seem willing to remain only as experiments outside formal systems.  
And at the end of that institutional shift, there may be a financial system that machines use more naturally than humans.

---

## The Defensive Instinct of Traditional Finance

The banking sector's pushback is strong.

There is concern that if stablecoins offer interest, or rewards similar to interest, bank deposits could flow out.  
Their fear is realistic to some extent.

At the same time, it also looks like the defensive instinct traditional finance often shows when facing a new wave.

They likely know this trend cannot be fully blocked.  
But perhaps they want to buy time until they are ready to stand at the center of the system,  
or at least secure a seat in the structure of interests.

The moment the floodgates fully open, they may change their tone faster than anyone.

"We are the safest stablecoin partner."

That scene does not feel unfamiliar.

---

## Crypto May Have Been Waiting for AI from the Start

The most interesting point is this.

Who will use this system best?

Traditional finance is built around human IDs, signatures, business hours, and approval flows.  
But crypto talks in code and keys.

Operating 24/7, repeating micro-payments, and automatically moving funds across countless wallets and contracts.

For humans, this structure can feel inconvenient and excessive.  
For AI agents, it may be a natural environment instead.

Maybe blockchain was never meant only as financial infrastructure for humans.

A base prepared in advance so future AI agents can store funds, pay, execute contracts, and trade with one another.  
Seen that way, many characteristics of crypto look different.

For AI, crypto can become the most natural system for controlling funds, almost a kind of financial native language.

---

## Why the U.S. Is Moving Quickly

I think the reason the U.S. is trying to organize a legal framework for digital assets is ultimately connected to this trend.

A powerful intelligence called AI is emerging.  
If that intelligence begins to work, decide, transact, and pay autonomously,  
financial infrastructure is essential underneath it.

It is unlikely the U.S. would leave that infrastructure operating on systems outside the U.S.

AI hegemony and dollar hegemony.  
Keeping those two together as one infrastructure without separating them.

I do not see the current CLARITY Act process as merely a coin regulation bill.  
I see it as preparation to bring the coming AI agent economy into a U.S.-led financial order.

That may be the real weight of tomorrow's decision.

---

## What Survives and What Disappears

Of course, not everything will survive this process.

Some projects will be integrated into mainstream infrastructure,  
some assets will be redefined in the language of regulated systems,  
and things without substance will disappear like dust.

The market will keep shaking through that process.

Some will tremble in fear,  
some will be swept up in euphoria,  
and some will once again speak with confidence too early.

But I want to look at this a little more calmly.

More important than the noise of price is the direction in which infrastructure is being laid.  
And the current flow, clearly, is moving toward integrating crypto not as a pure speculative asset,  
but as part of the next generation of financial systems.

---

## At an Inflection Point

The market turbulence may only be the cracking sound that comes as infrastructure settles into place.  
What matters is not being swept away by that noise, but not losing sight of the larger trend I am watching.

Prices can swing.  
Expectations can overheat, and disappointment may arrive faster than expected.

But what I need to watch is not short-term fluctuation, but the direction in which this infrastructure is being institutionalized.  
And within that flow, the quiet work of distinguishing what will survive and what will disappear.

I only need to move by the principles I set.  
Without getting excited too quickly, without being frightened too easily, and by steadily watching where the current of this era leads.

That's all for today's note. Looking forward to tomorrow's outcome...
