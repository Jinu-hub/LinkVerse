## After the Beta Release

I released the beta version of Market Memory on Vercel.

There are still fine adjustments to make.  
Mobile optimization needs more work, and there will surely be parts that need refining once I actually use it.

Even so, the features I wanted to prioritize in the MVP are nearly complete.

An idea that once existed only vaguely is now a service you can actually visit.  
At this stage, that alone feels like a meaningful milestone.

But what I want to organize in this post is not the implementation itself.

From here on, I want to think about telling people about it little by little —  
in other words, marketing.

---

## Where I'm Weak

To be honest, marketing is not an area I'm comfortable with.

Work like development or design — setting up structure and implementing piece by piece — comes relatively naturally.  
Defining problems, organizing data, building screens, and adding the features I need is familiar territory.

But taking what I've built out into the open,  
letting people know about it,  
and talking about it consistently — I'm still not used to that.

Maybe it's also the part I find tedious.

I end up wondering what to say and how,  
worrying about sounding too promotional,  
and if there's no response, it can quickly feel like it lost all meaning.

So I have no plan to start big from the beginning.

I'm not trying to build a grand campaign,  
run multiple channels at once,  
or design a complex funnel.

At this stage, I think what matters is not a perfect marketing strategy,  
but building a small rhythm I can keep up without overextending myself.

---

## Threads Only, for Now

The promotion channel I'm considering right now is just Threads.

There are other options — X, LinkedIn, Reddit, blog, newsletter, and more —  
but trying to handle every channel right away would clearly become too much.

Market Memory is still in beta,  
and it's a service I'm building and running on my own.

So for now, testing on one channel in a small way feels more realistic than spreading wide.

The reason I chose Threads is simple.

It's easy to post short updates,  
it doesn't feel too heavy,  
and it seems like a good fit for finding a tone between everyday notes and service introductions.

What I want to do right now is not hard selling.

Something like:

"There was this market movement,  
and Market Memory organized it this way."

I want to post bite-sized updates like that on a steady basis.

---

## The Basic Approach

I want to keep the approach simple.

On weekdays, I'll post one promotional update each day.  
But I won't write each post like a finished report.

The core idea is a taste, not the full meal.

I'll briefly summarize that day's market movement or main issue,  
and point readers to the Market Memory landing page or report page for more detail.

For example, something like this:

* A market flow worth paying attention to today
* One key change among AI, semiconductors, interest rates, exchange rates, or geopolitical issues
* How Market Memory interpreted that issue
* A note that the full details are available on the service

With this structure, I don't need to force out new promotional copy every time.

I can take the reports and summaries already being produced inside Market Memory  
and reshape them briefly for social media.

In other words, rather than making marketing a separate massive task,  
it's a way of bringing existing content outward little by little.

---

## Language Rotation

Market Memory supports Korean, Japanese, and English by default.

So rather than fixing promotional posts to one language,  
I want to rotate across all three.

I can't say from the start which language will get the best response.  
And since I'm running this alone, posting in every language every day would be too much.

So for now, I'm thinking of dividing languages across five weekdays.

An example flow might look like this.

Week 1

* Mon: English
* Tue: Korean
* Wed: Japanese
* Thu: English
* Fri: Korean

Week 2

* Mon: Japanese
* Tue: English
* Wed: Korean
* Thu: Japanese
* Fri: English

Week 3

* Mon: Korean
* Tue: Japanese
* Wed: English
* Thu: Korean
* Fri: Japanese

This way, I can avoid leaning too heavily on one language  
without having to write in all three every single day.

Of course, this rotation isn't a fixed rule.

If one language gets a better response, I can adjust the balance.  
If a particular market issue is more relevant to one region, I can flexibly switch the language that day.

What matters is not deciding the right answer from the start,  
but running it small and watching how people respond.

---

## Keeping Weekends Separate

For now, I'm thinking of weekends separately.

When the US market is closed, the flow of reports naturally differs from weekdays.  
Forcing daily market-following content through the weekend would only make the rhythm feel heavier.

So on weekends, rather than pushing daily promotional posts,  
it seems better to split them into a different format later.

For example, summarizing the major issues from the week,  
or looking back at what market movements Market Memory captured that week.

Nothing is decided yet,  
but weekends feel more natural as weekly wrap-ups or slightly more personal notes rather than daily promotion.

---

## Not Too Much Like Marketing

Ironically, the core of this marketing effort is to not make it feel too much like marketing.

I don't want to make the service look bigger than it is with exaggerated copy.  
Phrases like "the best investment report"  
or "perfectly analyzes the market" don't fit the direction I'm going for.

Market Memory is less a service that predicts the market for you,  
and more one that brings scattered market movements into a single line of sight.

That's the point I want to show.

Not asking people to take in every piece of market information,  
but calmly organizing what deserves to be at the center today.

I want to introduce it at that kind of temperature.

---

## Goals for Now

At this stage, the goals aren't big.

It's not about creating explosive traffic,  
or gathering a lot of users in a short time.

For now, the following would be enough.

* Post steadily on Threads on weekdays.
* Test the Korean, Japanese, and English rotation.
* See which language and which topics get a better response.
* Check whether the flow into the service feels natural.
* Build an operating rhythm I can keep up without strain.

Right now, rhythm comes before results.

Lowering marketing to a size I can handle,  
and instead building a structure that doesn't stop.

That feels like the approach that best fits Market Memory at the beta stage.

---

## Next Steps

What I need to do next is clear.

First, I'll put together a few post formats for Threads.

For example, a short market summary style,  
a format that goes deeper on one issue,  
or one that pulls out a striking line from a report.

After that, I need to settle on a natural tone for each language.  
Not too stiff, not drifting into overly business-like writing —  
I want to find a tone somewhere between a plain maker log and a market memo.

And I need to run it for a few weeks  
and see whether I can keep it up without overextending myself.

If marketing is ultimately part of the product,  
then it too won't be finished in one go —  
it'll be a process of building small, trying it, and adjusting along the way.

---

## A Short Reflection

Market Memory is now out in beta.

It's still too early to call it a finished service,  
but at least I've moved past the stage of keeping it only in my head.

What's needed now is telling people about it little by little.

I'm not comfortable with this part.  
There will probably be times when it still feels tedious.

Even so, this time I'm trying not to think too big.

One short post a day.  
One channel.  
A small rotation across three languages.  
And adjusting little by little as I watch the response.

For now, that's enough.

What matters is not packaging Market Memory with exaggeration,  
but bringing what I've built into the world at a rhythm I can actually sustain.
